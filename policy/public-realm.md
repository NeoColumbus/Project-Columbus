This is where myth becomes pavement.

## Position

Columbus should treat ordinary public space as civic architecture.

That means:

- safer crossings
- street trees that survive
- shade where people wait
- benches where bodies need rest
- lighting that helps without sterilizing
- riverfront access that belongs to daily life
- building fronts that acknowledge the sidewalk
- fewer dead edges around public buildings
- public art that does not feel purchased by committee

## The Test

Can a tired person sit?
Can a kid cross?
Can an elder wait?
Can a rider find the stop?
Can a stranger understand where they are?
Can the city be felt without spending money?

If not, the public realm is failing.
