# AI Civic Dividend

<!-- HUMAN NOTE: This is not anti-technology. This is anti-extraction. Keep the demand broad enough to inspire and specific enough to become policy. Legal, utility, tax, and procurement review required before public testimony. -->

If the machine lives here, it pays tribute to the city.

AI is not magic.

It is water.
Land.
Power.
Labor.
Tax policy.
Public infrastructure.
Public patience.
Human data.
Public knowledge.

Central Ohio is becoming AI infrastructure country.

That cannot mean private machines quietly consuming public inputs while Columbus gets a press release and a ribbon cutting.

The public should get a share.

## Position

Full City Columbus is not anti-AI.

This project is AI-assisted.

That makes the obligation sharper:

If AI helps build the future, the people whose water, power, land, data, and public systems make it possible deserve a civic dividend.

No invisible factories.
No black-box empire on public ground.
No household bill hikes for machine growth.
No tax break without public return.

## 1. Water Share

Data centers should disclose water use and cooling methods in plain public language.

The standard:

- public reporting of projected and actual water use
- public reporting of cooling technology
- drought and emergency plans
- reuse/reclaimed water where feasible
- limits on hidden freshwater draw
- discharge and heat-impact disclosure
- neighborhood notification before approvals

The line:

Our water is not background equipment.

## 2. Power Share

Data centers should pay for the grid they force into existence.

The standard:

- no residential or small-business cost shift
- large-load tariffs with real minimum commitments
- collateral before infrastructure is built
- exit fees when projects cancel after forcing grid planning
- public reporting of local grid impacts
- local resilience requirements
- clean power additions that are actually additive, not paper offsets

The line:

If the machine needs a city-sized appetite, it pays city-sized costs.

## 3. Land Share

A data center is heavy civic infrastructure.

It should be treated that way.

The standard:

- strict stormwater rules
- tree canopy and heat mitigation
- noise limits
- backup power disclosure
- emergency response planning
- no dead blank wall against active streets
- setbacks and screening
- construction traffic plans
- decommissioning plans
- reuse standards for sites that become obsolete

The line:

No dead boxes on civic ground.

## 4. Tax Share

If public revenue is waived, the city needs public return.

The standard:

- community-benefit agreements
- clawbacks for missed job, wage, or investment promises
- payments into housing, transit, water, and power resilience funds
- transparent tax-abatement dashboards
- local hiring and apprenticeship requirements
- local procurement targets
- public reporting of actual jobs, wages, and tax impact

The line:

No tax break without a body payment.

## 5. Compute Share

If Columbus hosts the machine, Columbus should get access to the machine.

The standard:

- cloud credits for Columbus schools
- AI tools and training for libraries
- compute grants for local artists, nonprofits, small businesses, and researchers
- apprenticeships for students and workers
- public-interest AI lab partnerships
- open-source civic tools
- free AI literacy programming in neighborhoods

The line:

Do not just store the future here. Let people use it.

## 6. Data Share

Public data should not become private extraction.

Columbus needs a privacy-first data commons: public where appropriate, protected where necessary, governed with public rules.

The standard:

- clear rules for public datasets used in AI systems
- no secret sale of public data
- privacy and security by design
- community review for sensitive datasets
- open data where release serves public benefit
- data trusts or civic data stewardship for high-value datasets
- algorithmic transparency for public-facing city systems
- opt-in models where residents are the source of data value

The line:

Our data is not free ore.

## 7. Neighborhood Share

When a data center lands near a community, that community gets more than traffic and hum.

The standard:

- sidewalks
- tree canopy
- parks
- utility relief
- noise mitigation
- construction accountability
- job pipelines
- small-business procurement
- community rooms or public tech labs
- neighborhood-specific benefit funds

The line:

If the machine has a footprint, the neighborhood gets a dividend.

## Policy Tests

Before supporting a data-center or AI infrastructure deal, ask:

1. What water does it use?
2. What power does it require?
3. Who pays for the grid?
4. What public revenue is waived?
5. What housing, transit, water, or neighborhood fund gets paid?
6. What compute access does Columbus get?
7. What data rights are protected?
8. What happens if the project cancels, shrinks, or becomes obsolete?

If those answers are vague, the deal is not ready.

## Public Message

Do not say:

- innovation district stakeholder synergy
- AI transformation ecosystem
- public-private partnership opportunity

Say:

AI is water, land, power, labor, and data.

If the machine lives here, it pays tribute to the city.

The future runs through Columbus.
Columbus gets a share.
